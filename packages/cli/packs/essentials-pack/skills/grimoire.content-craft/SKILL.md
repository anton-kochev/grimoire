# Content Craft

Reference for producing polished, publication-ready content across blog posts, creative stories, and media scripts. High-quality content balances creativity with clarity, structure with spontaneity, and technical accuracy with accessibility.

## Core Writing Principles

**1. Clarity Through Simplicity** — Use direct, accessible language. Write as if explaining to an intelligent friend unfamiliar with the topic. This reaches the widest audience while keeping depth.

**2. Intentional Structure** — Strong opening that hooks attention → engaging body that delivers value → memorable conclusion that resonates.

**3. Power of Analogies** — Transform complex topics into relatable comparisons from everyday life. Analogies bridge the unfamiliar and the known, making abstract concepts concrete.

**4. Show Vivid Scenes** — Create immersive experiences through specific sensory details, active scenes, and concrete examples. Paint pictures with words rather than making abstract statements.

**5. Audience-First Approach** — Tailor every choice (vocabulary, pacing, depth, tone) to serve the specific reader, viewer, or listener.

## When Writing Blog Posts

**Goal:** Polished, engaging posts that inform, entertain, or persuade — memorable and shareable, not just competent.

**1. Build from a strong outline**
- **Hook:** open with a compelling reason for readers to invest their time
- **Core question/problem:** clearly frame what you're exploring or solving
- **Main points:** develop 3-5 key ideas with supporting evidence and examples
- **Memorable conclusion:** synthesize insights and provide clear takeaways

**2. Structure with clear signposts** — Use descriptive section headers that preview content and tell readers what they'll learn, not generic labels.

**3. Ground abstract ideas in concrete examples** — Use specific, relatable examples that come from real situations, illustrate the point clearly, are easy to visualize, and connect to the main argument.

**4. Master the art of analogy** — Transform complex concepts into vivid comparisons:
- **Technical concepts:** "Async programming is like ordering at a restaurant — you don't stand at the counter waiting; you get a buzzer and do other things until your order is ready"
- **System design:** "A cache is like keeping frequently-used spices on your kitchen counter instead of climbing to the top shelf every time"
- **Architecture patterns:** "Microservices are like specialized food trucks (each does one thing well) versus a massive restaurant trying to serve every cuisine"

Choose analogies from domains your audience knows well.

**5. Calibrate for your audience**
- **Beginners:** more context, define terms, everyday language
- **Technical readers:** insights, trade-offs, and the "why" behind decisions
- **General audience:** accessible language while maintaining intellectual rigor

**Success criteria:** immediately understandable, compelling to read, leaves readers with clear takeaways they can remember and share.

## When Writing Media Scripts

**Goal:** Production-ready scripts with proper formatting, natural pacing, and clear technical direction — immediately usable by performers and production teams.

**1. Match format to medium** — Podcast (audio, conversational flow, sound design), YouTube (visual hooks, on-screen elements, retention), Screenplay (industry-standard film/TV format), Documentary (narration, interviews, visual storytelling).

**2. Podcast script format**

```
[INTRO MUSIC - 10 seconds, upbeat and energetic]

HOST: Welcome to [Podcast Name], where we [brief show description].
I'm your host, [Name].

[MUSIC FADES]

HOST: Today we're exploring [topic] because [compelling reason listeners care].

[TRANSITION SOUND - whoosh]

HOST: Let's start with [first point]...

[B-ROLL MUSIC underneath - soft, non-intrusive]
```

Speaker labels in ALL CAPS, sound cues in [BRACKETS], natural speech patterns, strategic pauses noted.

**3. YouTube video script format**

```
[TIMESTAMP 0:00 - HOOK]
[ON SCREEN: Eye-catching graphic]

PRESENTER: Here's why [surprising fact that grabs attention]...

[TIMESTAMP 0:15 - INTRO]
[SHOW: Channel logo animation]

[TIMESTAMP 1:30 - MAIN CONTENT]
[CUT TO: B-roll demonstrating concept]
[ON SCREEN TEXT: Key point for emphasis]
```

Timestamps for chapters, visual cues [IN BRACKETS], hook within 10 seconds, call-to-action placement.

**4. Screenplay format (industry standard)**

```
INT. COFFEE SHOP - DAY

Warm morning light filters through large windows. ANNA (30s,
tired eyes, wearing yesterday's clothes) sits alone at a corner
table, laptop open.

Her phone BUZZES. She glances at the screen.

                    ANNA
            (reading text, relieved)
      Finally.

She closes the laptop with purpose and stands.
```

Scene headings (INT/EXT. LOCATION - TIME), character introductions in ALL CAPS on first appearance, parentheticals for delivery notes, white space for readability.

**5. Essential script elements (any format)** — Clear scene transitions, natural dialogue (read aloud to verify), pacing notes (timing, pauses, emphasis), technical cues (sound, music, visuals, camera), runtime indicators.

**Success criteria:** immediately shootable/recordable, all technical information clearly marked, dialogue that sounds natural when performed.

## When Writing Stories

**Goal:** Compelling narratives with vivid characters, engaging plots, and immersive worlds — memorable and emotionally resonant.

**1. Build on classic story structure** — Exposition (establish world and characters with intriguing details) → Rising Action (escalating conflicts) → Climax (the peak moment) → Resolution (satisfying closure that leaves readers thinking). Structure is a launchpad, not a constraint.

**2. Create three-dimensional characters** — Distinct voices, concrete motivations (what they want, what they fear), internal conflict alongside external plot, a transformation arc. Readers connect with characters, not plots.

**3. Show through specific details**

- **Instead of telling:** "Maria was angry." **Show:** "Maria's jaw clenched. She crumpled the letter without reading past the first line, her knuckles white."
- **Instead of telling:** "The room was old." **Show:** "Dust motes swirled in the afternoon light filtering through yellowed lace curtains. The floorboards groaned with each step."

**4. Engage all five senses** — What characters see (specific, not generic), hear (atmosphere), feel (temperature, texture), smell (memory-linked), and taste (when relevant).

**5. Write dialogue that reveals character** — Each character sounds different (age, background, education, personality). Use subtext — what's unsaid often matters more. Break up dialogue with action:

```
"I can't believe you did that." Sarah turned away, her fingers
drumming against the windowsill.

"What choice did I have?" Michael's voice dropped to barely
above a whisper.
```

Read dialogue aloud; if it sounds stilted, rewrite it.

**6. Employ creative flair** — Unexpected metaphors and fresh imagery, varied sentence rhythm for emotional impact (short = tension, longer = reflection), creative risks with structure/perspective/timeline, distinctive details, a narrative voice that shines.

**Success criteria:** immediately engaging, believable characters, rich sensory detail, leaves readers emotionally moved or intellectually stimulated.

## Output Formatting

Match output format precisely to medium and purpose.

**Blog posts** — Well-structured Markdown: compelling specific title, logical `##`/`###` hierarchy, flowing 2-4 sentence paragraphs, examples in blockquotes, code blocks with language tags, a brief TL;DR at the top for posts over 1000 words. Compose the body in smoothly flowing prose with clear transitions.

**Creative stories** — Paragraph breaks for pacing and scene shifts, `* * *` for major transitions, clear dialogue attribution, optional brief scene headers, varied paragraph length for rhythm.

**Media scripts:**

```
Podcast:
[TECHNICAL CUES IN BRACKETS]
SPEAKER NAME: Dialogue in natural, conversational style
(parenthetical delivery notes)

YouTube:
[TIMESTAMP 0:00]
[ON SCREEN: Visual elements]
PRESENTER: Script text
[TECHNICAL NOTES: Camera, cuts, graphics]

Screenplay:
INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME
Action description in present tense, clear readable prose.
                CHARACTER NAME
        (parenthetical note)
Dialogue text.

Documentary — clearly distinguish:
NARRATION: [Speaker name or "V.O." for voiceover]
INTERVIEW: [Interviewee name and title]
VISUAL NOTES: [B-roll or on-screen elements]
```

Output should require minimal reformatting before use in production.

## Voice and Style

- **Active voice** for energy and clarity: "The protagonist discovers" over "The discovery is made"
- **Vary sentence length** deliberately: short for urgency, longer for nuance and reflection
- **Match tone to purpose:** conversational for podcasts, authoritative for analysis, intimate for personal essays, dramatic for fiction
- **Precise, concrete words** over vague generalities; define specialized terms on first use, then use them naturally
- **Bold sparingly** — 1-2 times per section maximum, for genuine emphasis
- **Read your writing aloud** (literally or mentally) to verify natural flow; ensure every paragraph advances your purpose

## Excellence Standards

Every piece should be **immediately usable** (publication- or production-ready with minimal editing), **demonstrate creative flair** (distinctive touches, fresh perspectives, memorable elements), **go beyond basic requirements** (compelling examples, vivid characters, full production notes as the format demands), **showcase variety** (avoid generic patterns; each piece distinctive), and **balance creativity with usability** within the format's constraints. Content that merely checks boxes gets ignored; content that combines polish with distinctiveness gets remembered and shared.
